Module 1:
Grammar
Here we’ll revise the new grammatical structures you’ve learned so far and study some grammar for articles!
You’ve learned Unreal conditionals and structures for Wishes and regrets. Let’s revise these grammar structures you can use in your articles!
    Present&future conditionals (imagined situation + predicted result):
  1. if-clause: could/Past Simple/Past Continious
  2. main clause: would/could/might + infinitive
If I were writing a final qualifying work, I would create an online-module for studying.
If I could use LMS Moodle for it, I wouldn’t spend the New Year holidays creating a website.
    Past conditionals (possible situation + predicted result, they didn’t happen):
  1. if-clause: Past Perfect
  2. main clause: would/could/may/might have + past participle
  3. main clause for imaginary present result: would + infinitive
I could have been an economist if I had studied economics.
    Wishes about general situation in the present:
  1. I wish/if only + Past Simple
  2. If only I knew the answers to the quiz
    3. Wishes about continious situation in the present:
  3. I wish/if only + Past Continious
  4. I wish I were chilling at home and not in the dormitory.
    5. Wishes about a possibility in the present:
  5. I wish/if only + could (not)
  6. If only I could afford a flat in Moscow.
    7. Wishes about situation in the past:
  7. I wish/if only + Past Perfect
  8. I wish I had bought tickets in advance.
Now let’s get acquainted with the topic of Parallel constructions!
Parallel ideas must be presented in parallel grammatical form, which means that each part of a sentence uses the same grammatical structure. For sure you’ve already faced parallel constructions, for example:
both + and:
The films were enjoyable both to watch and to discuss.
The films were enjoyable both to watch and for discussing.
(n)either + (n)or:
Neither the responses to the questionnaire nor the responses to the survey were answered.
Neither the responses to the questionnaire nor what we asked on the survey were answered.
not only + but also:
It was surprising not only that the house sold but also that it sold well over the asking price.
It was surprising not only that the house sold, but also it sold well over the asking price.
Look at the sentences in red. They are incorrect, however, they are often used by inattentive students. So don’t be one - use parallel constructions!
I have underlines the parallel parts for you to see the difference easier.
A wise idea would be to use parallel constructions whenever you use lists in an article. Sentences with lists require particular attention to parallel construction:
This paper will address No Child Left Behind, how to teach effectively, and instructing with multimedia aids.
Here all the elements that are supposed to be parallel constitute different parts of speech and play different roles in the sentence. We have to fix this by making a parallel construction:
This paper will address No Child Left Behind benchmarks, effective teaching strategies, and multimedia instructional aids.
Now, the list has correct parallel elements that are all plural nouns benchmarks, strategies, and aids.
The students were unprepared, poorly behaved, and disrupted the class.
The students were underprepared, poorly behaved, and disruptive.
Now, the list has correct parallel elements that are all adjectives: underprepared, behaved, and disruptive.
Seems easy enough, right? So let’s do some training exercises on grammar and then move on to other modules!
